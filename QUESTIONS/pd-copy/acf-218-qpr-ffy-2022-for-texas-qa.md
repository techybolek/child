# Texas FFY 2022 Quality Progress Report - Question & Answer Pairs

## Q1: What is the Quality Progress Report (QPR) and what time period does it cover?
**A1:** The Quality Progress Report (QPR) is an annual report that collects information from lead agencies to describe investments made to improve the quality of child care available for children from birth to age 13. The report meets requirements in the Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG) Act of 2014 for lead agencies to describe how quality funds were expended, including the activities funded and measures used to evaluate progress. This specific report covers federal fiscal year activities from October 1, 2021 through September 30, 2022.

## Q2: How many child care providers were operating in Texas as of September 30, 2022?
**A2:** As of September 30, 2022, Texas had 9,604 licensed center-based programs, 21 license-exempt center-based programs, 4,177 licensed family child care homes, and 4 license-exempt family child care homes. In-home care providers (care in the child's own home) are not regulated or tracked in Texas.

## Q3: What is the Texas Workforce Registry and how many people are using it?
**A3:** The Texas Workforce Registry is a web-based application for early childhood education (ECE) professionals who work with children birth to age eight. It helps track ECE professionals' education, employment history, and training hours. As of September 30, 2022, 61,252 practitioners and 3,919 directors were utilizing the registry in some capacity. While statewide participation is voluntary, all certified Texas Rising Star programs are required to have at least a director participate in the registry as of January 2021.

## Q4: What types of workforce development supports were provided to child care providers in FFY 2022?
**A4:** Texas provided several workforce development supports including scholarships for formal education institutions (1,292 recipients), financial bonuses/wage supplements tied to education levels (161 recipients), career advisors/mentors/coaches/consultants (2,025 recipients), reimbursement for training (174 recipients), financial bonuses not tied to education levels (6,863 recipients), and salary enhancements/wage supplements (38 recipients). These supports helped providers progress in their education, professional development, and career pathways.

## Q5: What is Texas Rising Star and what progress was made in FFY 2022?
**A5:** Texas Rising Star is the state's Quality Rating and Improvement System (QRIS) that operates statewide. Providers that achieve Texas Rising Star certification offer high-quality care exceeding minimum standards for director/staff qualifications, teacher-child interactions, curricula, environments, program administration, and parent involvement. The program has three levels of certification: Two-Star, Three-Star, and Four-Star. In FFY 2022, Texas saw a 4% increase in the number of Texas Rising Star programs, with 28% of providers receiving subsidies (2,025 out of 7,244 subsidized providers) participating in the program.

## Q6: How much CCDF quality set-aside funding was spent on training and professional development of the child care workforce?
**A6:** Texas spent $10,220,887 from CCDF quality set-aside funds on training and professional development of the child care workforce during FFY 2022. This included statewide initiatives such as the Professional Development Scholarship initiative and supporting the Texas Workforce Registry, as well as local Board initiative spending like conferences, training sessions, college scholarships, and CDA stipends. Additionally, $50,981 in CARES Act funds was used for the Child Care Apprenticeship initiative.

## Q7: What is the Professional Development Scholarship initiative and what were its outcomes in FFY 2022?
**A7:** The Professional Development Scholarship initiative provides funding to T.E.A.C.H. Early Childhood® TEXAS, which offers scholarships for those working in licensed child care centers and registered or licensed family child care homes. CCDF-funded scholarships cover CDA assessment fees, associate and bachelor degrees, and registered apprenticeship programs. In FFY 2022, TWC added $3.5 million of ARPA discretionary funding, resulting in 152 CDA scholarships, 236 Associate scholarships, 49 Bachelor scholarships, and 47 apprenticeship participants—a 61% increase in total participants supported compared to the previous year.

## Q8: What complaints and inspections activity occurred in Texas child care programs during FFY 2022?
**A8:** During FFY 2022, Child Care Regulation received 10,715 complaints regarding licensed providers and 0 complaints regarding license-exempt providers. The average response time to complaints was 2.18 days. Of these complaints, 5,851 received an on-site follow-up inspection, and 2,903 resulted in one or more substantiated violations. Seven child care providers closed as a result of inspections during this period. All child care programs (9,604 licensed centers, 4,177 licensed family homes, and 4 license-exempt family homes) received coaching or technical assistance to improve their understanding and adherence to CCDF health and safety standards.

## Q9: How many child care programs participated in infant and toddler quality improvement activities?
**A9:** Texas supported significant infant and toddler quality activities in FFY 2022. There were 386 infant-toddler specialists available to all providers and to CCDF providers, serving 2,711 providers and reaching 7,892 children. Additionally, 264 provider staff were trained by local Boards on the infant and toddler guidelines, and 1,926 providers met Texas Rising Star standards for infant/toddler and prekindergarten guidelines. The state also created 111 new infant slots and 226 new toddler slots during this period.

## Q10: What were the Child Care Relief Funds (CCRF) and how were they used to stabilize the child care sector?
**A10:** The Child Care Relief Funds (CCRF) were grants provided using COVID-19 stimulus funding (CARES, CRRSA, and ARPA) to support eligible regulated child care providers. Beginning in June 2021, TWC awarded 9,479 providers with these funds, with 1,116 providers paid during FFY 2022 specifically (907 licensed center-based programs and 209 licensed family child care homes). The ARPA-funded CCRF 2022 provided quarterly payments to providers as long as they remained open and in good standing. TWC established robust fraud prevention measures including data verification against Child Care Regulation databases, independent quality control reviews, Internal Audit reviews, and contracted with KPMG to audit approximately 25% of CCRF beneficiaries. TWC also approved $20 million of ARPA funds to support monitoring and oversight, including a CCRF fraud hotline.
