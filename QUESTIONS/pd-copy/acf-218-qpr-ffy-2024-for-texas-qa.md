# Quality Progress Report (QPR) FFY 2024 - Texas
## Question-Answer Pairs

**Q1: What is the Quality Progress Report (QPR) and what time period does it cover?**

A1: The Quality Progress Report (QPR) is an annual report that lead agencies must submit to describe investments made to improve the quality of care available for children from birth to age 13. The report describes how quality funds were expended, including activities funded and measures used to evaluate progress in improving child care program quality. This report covers federal fiscal year 2024 (October 1, 2023, through September 30, 2024).

**Q2: How many licensed child care providers operated in Texas as of September 30, 2024?**

A2: As of September 30, 2024, Texas had 10,496 licensed center-based programs and 4,228 licensed family child care homes operating in the state. These counts include all licensed providers, not just those serving children receiving CCDF subsidies. Of these, 6,893 licensed center-based programs and 491 licensed family child care homes were receiving CCDF funding.

**Q3: What is the Texas Workforce Registry and how many people are using it?**

A3: The Texas Workforce Registry is a web-based application for early childhood education (ECE) professionals who work with children birth to age eight. It allows ECE professionals to track their education, employment history, and training hours. As of September 30, 2024, 81,413 total participants were utilizing the registry, including 76,482 practitioners and 4,931 administrators. Participation is voluntary statewide, but programs participating in Texas Rising Star are required to have at least a program director account.

**Q4: What types of professional development support did Texas provide to child care workers in FFY 2024?**

A4: Texas provided multiple forms of professional development support including: 1,779 scholarships for formal education institutions; 2,206 financial bonuses/wage supplements tied to education levels; 511 reimbursements for training; and mentoring for all 8,053 programs participating in the Child Care Services (CCS) subsidy program. The state spent a total of $27,559,910 across all funding sources to support training and professional development of the child care workforce.

**Q5: What is Texas Rising Star and how many programs participate in it?**

A5: Texas Rising Star is Texas's quality rating and improvement system (QRIS) with four tiers: Entry Level and three star-level certifications (Two-Star, Three-Star, and Four-Star). Programs that achieve Two-Star certification or higher demonstrate high-quality care exceeding minimum standards for staff qualifications, teacher-child interactions, curricula, environment, administration, and parent involvement. As of FFY 2024, 48% of providers receiving subsidies participated as certified providers (3,843 Texas Rising Star certified providers out of 8,053 subsidized providers), representing a 36% increase in certified programs from FY 2023.

**Q6: How does Texas support infant and toddler care quality?**

A6: Texas supports infant and toddler care through multiple initiatives: the Texas Infant-Toddler Specialist Network with 573 specialists serving 7,713 providers and reaching 62,386 children; two Family Child Care Networks that served 119 home-based providers in FY 2024; creation of 226 new infant slots and 309 new toddler slots; and a total investment of $25,007,910 above and beyond the required 3% infant and toddler set-aside. Additionally, the Child Care Expansion Initiative resulted in 14,848 new infant slots among 51,981 total new slots created.

**Q7: What is the T.E.A.C.H. Early Childhood® TEXAS scholarship program and what were its outcomes in FY 2024?**

A7: T.E.A.C.H. Early Childhood® TEXAS is TWC's Professional Development Scholarship initiative that provides scholarships to child care professionals working in licensed centers and registered or licensed family child care homes. CCDF-funded scholarships are available for CDA assessment fees, associate and bachelor's degrees, and registered apprenticeship programs. In FY 2024, the program awarded 764 CDA scholarships, 632 Associate scholarships, 189 Bachelor scholarships, and supported 245 apprenticeship participants—a 68% increase in total participants from the previous year.

**Q8: How much did Texas spend on facilitating compliance with health, safety, and licensing standards in FFY 2024?**

A8: Texas spent $19,264,403 across all funding sources on facilitating compliance with health, safety, and licensing standards during FFY 2024. This included supporting 326 licensing staff positions, responding to 11,061 complaints about providers, and providing coaching or technical assistance to 9,784 child care programs to improve their understanding and adherence to CCDF health and safety standards. The state also used funding to support activities like First Aid/CPR training for 1,958 staff members and providing equipment like AED machines to 170 providers.

**Q9: What was the Child Care Relief Fund (CCRF) and how much funding did Texas distribute?**

A9: The Child Care Relief Fund (CCRF) was Texas's stabilization grant program approved in October 2021 using $2.45 billion in American Rescue Plan Act Stabilization funds, with an additional $1 billion approved in February 2022 from CARES and ARPA Discretionary funds, totaling $3.45 billion. Funds were distributed in four quarterly payments beginning in March 2022, with providers serving children with scholarships receiving a 5th payment. The deadline to expend CCRF funding was November 30, 2023. In FFY 2024, 457 licensed center-based programs and 82 licensed family child care homes received sustainability funding.

**Q10: What new water safety rules did Texas adopt in 2024 and what do they require?**

A10: In September 2024, Texas Child Care Regulation adopted new water safety rules implementing HB 59 from the 88th legislative session. Key requirements include: parents must indicate at admission whether their child can swim competently or requires a personal flotation device (PFD); water safety training is required for center directors, home primary caregivers, and employees who accompany children to pools; and before allowing children who cannot swim competently to enter a pool, centers must provide, ensure proper fitting, and ensure the child wears a Type I, II, or III U.S. Coast Guard approved PFD in good repair.
