# Texas Quality Progress Report (QPR) FFY 2023 - Questions & Answers

## Q1: What is the Quality Progress Report (QPR) and what time period does it cover?
**A1:** The Quality Progress Report (QPR) is an annual report required by the Child Care and Development Block Grant (CCDBG) Act of 2014 that describes how quality funds were expended, including the activities funded and the measures used to evaluate progress in improving the quality of child care programs and services. The FFY 2023 report covers federal fiscal year activities from October 1, 2022, through September 30, 2023, and was accepted on April 9, 2024.

## Q2: How many licensed child care providers were operating in Texas as of September 30, 2023?
**A2:** As of September 30, 2023, Texas had 9,779 licensed center-based programs and 4,024 licensed family child care homes operating in the state. According to the FY 2023 ACF-800 data, 6,454 of the licensed center-based programs and 556 of the licensed family child care homes were receiving CCDF funding.

## Q3: What is the Texas Workforce Registry and who is required to participate?
**A3:** The Texas Workforce Registry is a web-based application for early childhood education (ECE) professionals who work with children birth to age eight that allows practitioners to track their education, employment history, and training hours. As of September 30, 2023, 87,605 practitioners and 5,006 directors were utilizing the registry. While statewide participation is voluntary, effective January 2021, child care programs participating in Texas Rising Star are required to have, at minimum, a program director account within the Texas Workforce Registry.

## Q4: What are the four tiers of the Texas Rising Star quality rating system and what do they represent?
**A4:** Texas Rising Star has four tiers: Entry Level designation (an entry point allowing programs up to 24 months to achieve certification), Two-Star certification (scoring below 1.80 or less than 60% of total possible points), Three-Star certification (scoring 1.80 to 2.39 or 60-79.9% of total points), and Four-Star certification (scoring 2.4 or greater or 80% of total points). The rating system uses a hybrid scoring methodology with four certification categories, with Teacher-Child Interactions weighted at 40% and the remaining three categories weighted at 20% each. Only the Two-, Three-, and Four-Star levels are considered "high quality."

## Q5: How much did Texas spend on professional development and training for the child care workforce in FFY 2023?
**A5:** Texas spent a total of $16,501,825 across all funding sources to support the training and professional development of the child care workforce during FFY 2023. This included statewide initiatives such as the Professional Development Scholarship initiative and supporting the Texas Workforce Registry, as well as local Board initiative spending like conferences, training sessions, college scholarships, and CDA stipends. The state used CCDF quality funds, CRRSA funds, and ARPA Supplemental funds for these activities.

## Q7: How many child care programs participated in Texas Rising Star in FFY 2023, and how did this compare to previous years?
**A7:** In FY 2023, Texas saw a 28% increase in the number of Texas Rising Star programs compared to FY 2022. As of the reporting period, 2,829 programs were certified at star levels (2,509 licensed center-based programs and 320 licensed family child care homes), serving 110,931 children. The distribution across star levels was: 316 Two-Star programs, 1,071 Three-Star programs, and 1,742 Four-Star programs. Additionally, 37% of providers receiving subsidies participated in Texas Rising Star as a certified provider.

## Q8: What was the Child Care Relief Fund (CCRF) 2022 and how much funding did Texas distribute to providers?
**A8:** The Child Care Relief Fund (CCRF) 2022 was Texas's implementation of American Rescue Plan Act Stabilization funds and additional stimulus funds to support child care providers. On October 19, 2021, TWC approved $2.45 billion in ARPA Stabilization funds, and on February 1, 2022, approved an additional $1 billion from CARES and ARPA Discretionary funds, bringing the total to $3.45 billion. Applications were accepted from March through May 31, 2022, with funds distributed in four quarterly payments beginning in March 2022. Providers serving children with scholarships received a 5th payment to support the new Texas Rising Star participation requirement. As of the reporting period, 6,476 licensed center-based programs and 1,448 licensed family child care homes received these grants.

## Q9: What is the Child Care Expansion Initiative (CCEI) and what were its outcomes as of September 30, 2023?
**A9:** The Child Care Expansion Initiative (CCEI) offers start-up funding to help expand child care availability in child care deserts, support programs opening or expanding in partnership with local employers, and expand infant care capacity around the state. As of September 30, 2023, TWC had awarded 394 start-up awards totaling $82.5 million and $47.7 million in Initial Operating awards to 402 providers. By November 30, 2023, TWC had awarded over $183 million to 902 providers to create 50,537 new child care seats, including 16,241 new infant seats. Of these, 130 providers expanded in child care deserts, 260 providers increased infant capacity only, and 512 providers expanded in partnership with one or more employers.

## Q10: How did Texas support compliance with licensing, health, and safety standards in FFY 2023?
**A10:** Texas spent $25,287,418 across all funding sources on facilitating compliance with lead agency requirements for inspections, monitoring, health and safety standards and training, and licensing standards during FFY 2023. Child Care Regulation had 328 licensing staff positions and conducted 19,459 application and monitoring inspections (including listed homes), 6,866 follow-up inspections, and 6,522 investigation inspections. Additionally, 10,674 child care programs received coaching or technical assistance to improve their understanding and adherence to CCDF health and safety standards as a result of an inspection or violation. The state also received 11,038 complaints regarding providers during this period and used CCDF quality funds, CRRSA funds, and ARPA Supplemental Discretionary funds to support these activities.
